# Design Note

Executive search is not a summarisation problem. It is a decision-support problem under uncertainty. In PPP's world, the real task is not to produce five neat profile write-ups. It is to help a consultant decide, from uneven public evidence, who deserves the first call, who belongs in a second-wave screen, and who should stay in the market map. Public signals are patchy, identity confidence is uneven, and remit shape is often only partially visible. That means the system has to support call-priority judgment without pretending the evidence is cleaner than it is.

The system plugs directly into the existing consultant workflow: mandate intake, candidate review, call-priority decision, then live screening. It does not introduce a new process; it compresses the one already in use. The role specification anchors what the mandate needs, and each candidate is normalized into a structured evidence profile before the final brief is written. The middle step in the workflow is not “write a nice summary,” but “decide who I call first and what I need to test on that call.” The output is therefore meant to behave like a recruiter decision artifact, not a generic résumé synopsis.

One of the most important design choices is that the system does not stop at brief generation. It explicitly structures relative priority. The ranking and score are explicitly designed to express phone-call order, not just a vague label such as “good” or “relevant.” In practice, the output answers a more operational question: if several people are worth screening, who is the most commercially sensible first call, who is the next one, and who is credible but lower urgency. This makes prioritisation explicit rather than inferred.

The operational value is time compression. First, it reduces first-pass research time by converting raw candidate inputs and public evidence into a consistent recruiter-ready shape. Second, it reduces comparison effort across the five-person slate by enforcing the same role-fit dimensions and output structure across every candidate. Third, it lowers the cognitive load of uncertainty handling by carrying evidence distinctions directly into the output. Instead of asking the consultant to remember which candidate was verified, which was only a possible match, and which looked senior but still had unclear channel transferability, the system makes those differences explicit. This shifts the consultant’s time from basic information gathering to higher-value judgment and conversation.

Auditability is explicit. The structured JSON output is the review artifact. Evidence normalization produces recruiter-facing signals and confidence states that can be inspected rather than hidden inside fluent prose. This makes the reasoning behind each ranking inspectable rather than implicit. The same normalized evidence informs score, rationale, and outreach posture, reducing drift between narrative and ranking. A QA layer runs before export so the final bundle is valid and internally consistent. Fixture-backed reproducibility provides a stable review path even when public evidence changes.

That evidence discipline shapes the trade-offs. Stronger public support allows a more assertive shortlist-style note, while weaker identity confidence remains cautious. Fixture mode is therefore the preferred review path because it stabilizes the evidence pack and exported artifact, while live mode still demonstrates that the workflow can operate against changing public sources.

A consultant would use this because it does not change their workflow, only compresses it. It improves consistency without removing judgment. The consultant still decides whom to call, how to interpret nuance, and what to test live. The system’s role is to make that judgment faster, more comparable across candidates, and more disciplined about what the public record actually supports. In practice, this makes the output easier to trust, not just faster to produce.
